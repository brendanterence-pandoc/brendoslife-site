Of someone in the room who could explain to a $400M-revenue customer why an Enterprise Agreement was the right shape for them, or why it was not. I read that piece in early 2002. I had spent the previous year, with a small team inside Microsoft Worldwide Licensing, designing the ESA architecture Diamond was describing. We were trying to solve a specific problem. Enterprise customers were buying volume licenses they did not understand from resellers who were not paid to explain them. Twenty-four years later, Microsoft is unwinding that architecture. The 2026 commercial transition eliminates roughly $2.5 billion in partner compensation that flowed through the LSP and ESA structures and replaces it with the Modern Commerce model and the CSP path. The CMA opened a Strategic Market Status investigation into Microsoft's business software ecosystem on May 14. The comment window closes June 4. The Register published a piece I wrote this week on the architectural mechanics. The structural argument lives there. This piece is for the solution provider community CRN serves. It is about what the 2026 unwind means if you are a partner trying to figure out where your business model goes next. Three points. One. The advisory fee was a price signal, not a margin grab. The original ESA design paid solution providers for time spent advising customers on the right contracting vehicle, the right SKU mix, the right renewal posture. That money came out of the customer's net price, but it came out for a reason. Someone had to do the work. When Microsoft removes the advisory fee and replaces it with a transaction margin model, the work does not stop being necessary. It stops being compensated. Solution providers who have been making the work happen anyway, on the assumption that the relationship is the asset, are about to find out what the relationship is worth without a fee structure attached. Two. The 2026 transition has no partner bridge. In 2001, when ESA went live, we built a transition path. Partners who had been operating under the prior model had a defined window to migrate their advisory practice into the new structure. That window was generous because the architecture depended on partner capability surviving the change. In 2026 there is no equivalent bridge. The LSP-to-CSP shift is being treated as a margin recalibration when it is, in practice, a different commercial model with different unit economics. A partner whose 2025 P&L was built on LSP commissions does not automatically have a 2027 P&L built on CSP margins. The math does not carry across. Three. The 1998 DOJ antitrust framing has a 2026 echo. In the late 1990s, the question regulators kept asking Microsoft was whether the company's platform power was being used to dictate the economics of the ecosystems around it. The eventual settlement turned partly on whether independent commercial actors had enough latitude to operate without being structurally captured. The CMA SMS investigation is, in form, a different inquiry. A UK regulatory body asking whether Microsoft's position in business software justifies a strategic market designation. The substance rhymes. If a $2.5B partner compensation pool is dismantled and the partners holding the contracts do not have a meaningful alternative path, the structural-capture question is back on the table. For solution providers reading this, three practical postures are worth considering. First, audit your 2025 revenue by program. The partners I have spoken with who are clear-eyed about the unwind are the ones who already know what percentage of their 2025 revenue came from LSP commissions versus MCA-E margin versus CSP transactional. The partners who will be hit hardest are the ones who never broke their P&L down that way. Second, price your advisory work explicitly.
